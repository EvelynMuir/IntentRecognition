\cresp{There should be larger-scale datasets to better evaluate the method.}
{We added a generalization study on the EMOTIC affect dataset, detailed in our
response to Reviewer~2 comment~(c). Briefly: on EMOTIC's standard split the
agreement gate reduces to standard distillation \emph{by construction}, because
EMOTIC multi-annotates only val/test while its training split is single-annotator
(degenerate agreement); we therefore ran a controlled $5\times5$ cross-validation
on the multi-annotated val+test pool, where per-sample agreement is available.
There, privileged distillation \emph{significantly} improves the image-only
baseline on every metric (mAP/Macro/Micro/Samples-F1, paired $p<0.01$ over $25$
runs; Table~\ref{tab:emotic_pool} in the Reviewer~2 response), giving positive
evidence that the framework transfers to a second, affect domain. We report the
gate's incremental effect transparently (it matches standard KD on this pool,
where the borrowed agreement is coarser than Intentonomy's) and state the
cross-task scope as a limitation (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).}

\cresp{Experimental evaluation is not sufficient; more comparisons with 2025/2026
LLM methods and t-SNE are needed.}
{We added a zero-shot MLLM comparison (E5, Table~\ref{tab:main_results},
``Zero-shot VLM'' group) using
the same Intentonomy label set and probability-dictionary prompting protocol.
GPT-4o reaches Test Macro/Micro/Samples/Avg.\ F1 of
\res{43.05, 54.77, 56.75, and 51.52}. We also evaluated two newer open-weight MLLMs
through vLLM services: Qwen3-VL-8B-Instruct obtains Test
\res{38.51, 40.75, 39.63, and 39.63}, and InternVL3-8B obtains
\res{37.54, 41.85, 40.50, and 39.97}. These direct-prompting baselines are
competitive but still below the trained CLIP/FDIL models, supporting the need
for dataset-specific visual calibration and uncertainty-aware refinement. We
also added t-SNE visualizations of intent embeddings for FDIL vs.\ the baseline,
emphasizing hard / semantically adjacent classes (E6, Figure~\ref{fig:tsne}).}

\cresp{Add more theoretical analysis, e.g.\ from information-theoretic or
feature-selection perspectives.}
{Added a formal analysis in \S\ref{sec:decoupling_principle}. We decompose the
per-class excess risk into a label-side conditional-entropy term (supervisory
ambiguity, a functional of $\Pr(Y\mid X)$) and a feature-side inter-class
mutual-information aliasing term (semantic ambiguity, a functional of the
representation $\phi$), and show the two corrections have non-interfering
first-order effects -- SLR-C is a feature-selection step that lowers the aliasing
term, while UTD's agreement gate reduces reliance on high-entropy targets. The
analysis yields three testable predictions that the experiments confirm
(additive threshold-free increments, content-dependence of the rationale teacher,
and non-uniqueness of the decoupled realization). See also Reviewer~2 comment~(a).}

\cresp{A comparative analysis of model parameters, FLOPs, or inference times is
necessary.}
{We report exact, same-environment params/FLOPs/latency for the components we
control (Table~\ref{tab:efficiency_comparison}) and added a paragraph placing
FDIL's overhead against the external methods (E8, Section~\ref{sec:experiments},
Efficiency Comparison). A fully matched table across these methods is not
meaningful because they assume incomparable backbones and inference regimes --
HLEG/IntCLIP use convolutional or smaller encoders, while IntentMLM runs a
multimodal LLM with caption generation and retrieval (billions of parameters,
tens-to-hundreds of milliseconds per image). Relative to all of them, FDIL adds
only $2.41$M inference-active parameters and sub-millisecond feature-level latency
on top of the shared frozen CLIP encoder, which we state explicitly rather than
fabricate matched FLOPs for incomparable pipelines.}
