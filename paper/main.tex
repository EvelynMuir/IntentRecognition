%% Abstract
\begin{abstract}
%% Text of abstract
Visual intention understanding aims to infer the latent purpose conveyed by an image, and is fundamentally more challenging than conventional image classification due to its abstract and subjective nature. A central difficulty of this task lies in two distinct forms of ambiguity: supervisory ambiguity, caused by subjective annotation over plausible intentions, and semantic ambiguity, caused by semantically adjacent and overlapping intents.
In this work, we propose SADIR (\textbf{Stage-Aware Disambiguation for Intent Recognition}), a framework that treats ambiguity differently across stages: training focuses on stabilizing supervision under disagreement, while inference focuses on discriminating semantically adjacent candidates. To address supervisory ambiguity, we introduce Uncertainty-guided Text Distillation (UTD), which converts annotator disagreement into an adaptive semantic regularization signal. To address semantic ambiguity, we further propose Semantic Local Reranking and Calibration (SLR-C), which performs lightweight reranking within a compact Top-K candidate set using heterogeneous textual priors. Extensive experiments on the Intentonomy dataset demonstrate that SADIR consistently improves both hard-sample robustness and overall multi-label prediction performance over strong baselines and recent state-of-the-art methods.
\end{abstract}
