\cresp{There should be larger-scale datasets to better evaluate the method.}
{We have added a generalization study on the EMOTIC affect dataset, developed in full in our response to Reviewer~2, comment~(c). In brief, EMOTIC's standard split is uninformative for our agreement gate by construction, because the dataset multi-annotates only its validation and test splits, so every single-annotator training instance carries a degenerate agreement and the gated term reduces to standard distillation. We therefore evaluated the framework in the regime where its precondition holds, running a controlled $5\times5$ cross-validation on the multi-annotated validation+test pool, in which per-sample agreement is available. There, privileged distillation \emph{significantly} improves the image-only baseline on every metric (mAP, Macro-, Micro-, and Samples-F1; paired $p<0.01$ over $25$ runs; Table~\ref{tab:emotic_pool} in the Reviewer~2 response), providing positive evidence that the framework transfers to a second, affect domain. Where agreement is present the gate also activates and, consistent with its design, concentrates its benefit on low-agreement samples; in aggregate it matches standard distillation on this pool, whose borrowed agreement signal is coarser than Intentonomy's, and we report this scope transparently in the Limitations (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).}

\cresp{Add more theoretical analysis, e.g.\ from information-theoretic or feature-selection perspectives.}
{We have added a formal analysis in \S\ref{sec:decoupling_principle}, motivated by an empirical diagnostic showing why a feature-selection / local-aliasing view is appropriate: the visual baseline's Top-10 candidates already cover \res{$83.9\%$} of positive labels, \res{$95.1\%$} of test images contain at least one ground-truth intent inside that Top-10 set, and \res{$67.8\%$} of baseline false negatives are still recoverable from the same candidate set. An oracle restricted to this local neighborhood would reach \res{$87.4/91.3/89.7$} Macro/Micro/Samples F1. This indicates that the core semantic failure is often not global absence of the correct intent, but local ranking, calibration, and separation among already retrieved, semantically adjacent candidates. Based on this observation, we decompose the per-class excess risk into a label-side conditional-entropy term (supervisory ambiguity, a functional of $\Pr(Y\mid X)$) and a feature-side inter-class mutual-information aliasing term (semantic ambiguity, a functional of the representation $\phi$), and show the two corrections have non-interfering first-order effects -- SLR-C is a feature-selection step that lowers the aliasing term, while UTD's agreement gate reduces reliance on high-entropy targets. The analysis yields three testable predictions that the experiments confirm (additive threshold-free increments, content-dependence of the rationale teacher, and non-uniqueness of the decoupled realization). See also Reviewer~2 comment~(a).}

\cresp{A comparative analysis of model parameters, FLOPs, or inference times is necessary.}
{We now report exact, same-environment params/FLOPs/latency for all methods that admit a feature-level head, including the external baselines (Table~\ref{tab:efficiency_comparison}). Using their public source code, we profile HLEG and IntCLIP under the same shared frozen CLIP ViT-L/14 protocol as their ``(CLIP ViT-L/14)'' accuracy rows, counting each method's inference-active head on a single RTX 4090. The contrast is informative: HLEG's Query2Label transformer decoder alone costs $203.7$M params, $12.1$G FLOPs, and $2.73$ms per image, whereas IntCLIP/DualCoOp's learnable head is only $0.02$M prompt-context parameters ($22.0$M FLOPs, $0.11$ms) because its classifier weights are cached frozen-text-encoder embeddings. FDIL sits between them at $2.41$M params, $4.93$M FLOPs, and $0.47$ms. IntentMLM is the only method kept qualitative: it runs a multimodal LLM with caption generation and retrieval (billions of parameters, tens-to-hundreds of milliseconds per image), whose cost is dominated by the LLM rather than a feature-level head. See Section~\ref{sec:experiments} (Efficiency Comparison).}
