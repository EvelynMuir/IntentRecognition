\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\subsubsection{Dataset and Evaluation Metrics}
We evaluate our method on the Intentonomy dataset \cite{jia_intentonomy_2021}, a multi-label benchmark for visual intention recognition in social media scenarios. Following the standard split used in our experiments, the dataset contains 12,739 training images, 498 validation images, and 1,216 test images. Intentonomy additionally provides soft agreement annotations with values in $\{0, 0.33, 0.66, 1\}$ on the training set, which directly reflect the degree of annotator consensus and serve as the uncertainty signal $\omega$ in our framework.

We use Macro F1, Micro F1, Samples F1 as the evaluation metrics. To be consistent with recent Intentonomy works, we additionally report $\textit{Avg.}$, which is the average of three F1 scores. 

To better understand the effectiveness of our method, we further conduct experiments on subsets of the intent classes. We follow the work of Jia et al. \cite{jia_intentonomy_2021} and break down intent classes according to content difficulty and dependency. Difficulty classes are measured by how much a standard ResNet-50 model outperforms the random results. Content classes are divided into object-dependent ({\itshape Object}-classes), context-dependent ({\itshape Context}-classes), and {\itshape Others}, which depend on both foreground and background information. We emphasize the \textit{Hard} score throughout the analysis because it specifically measures performance on ambiguity-prone intent categories, where semantic overlap and subjective disagreement are most severe. Improvements on the hard subset therefore, provide stronger evidence that a method is resolving the core difficulty of intention understanding rather than merely exploiting easy classes.

\subsubsection{Implementation Details}

Our default visual backbone is frozen CLIP ViT-L/14 \cite{radford_learning_2021}. The visual student is implemented as a lightweight residual MLP trained on top of a fixed SLR-C prior. In SLR-C, priors are constructed from three sources, namely lexical intent phrases, canonical intent descriptions, and Gemini 3.1 Pro-generated scenario descriptions \cite{team_gemini_2025} with frozen CLIP text encoder \cite{radford_learning_2021}; their normalized logits are averaged before local reranking within \revised{Top-10} visual candidates. The scenario-prompt template is also provided in \ref{app:scenario_prompt}. For UTD, rationale text is generated offline by Qwen2.5-VL-7B-Instruct \cite{bai_qwen25-vl_2025} and then encoded by a frozen bge-large-en-v1.5 text encoder \cite{xiao_c-pack_2024}. The rationale-generation prompt template is provided in \ref{app:rationale_prompt}. The teacher consumes only the resulting text embeddings and is optimized with Asymmetric Loss (ASL) \cite{ridnik_asymmetric_2021} using $\gamma_{-}=2$, $\gamma_{+}=0$, and clipping value 0.05. The student is optimized with AdamW \cite{loshchilov_decoupled_2018}, learning rate $5\times10^{-4}$, weight decay $10^{-4}$, batch size 256, and dropout 0.1. The teacher's hidden dimension is 1,024, and the student's hidden dimension is 768. The distillation temperature is set to 2.0. During inference, class-wise thresholds are searched on the validation set and then fixed for test evaluation. Notably, UTD introduces \emph{zero extra inference cost}, since the text teacher is only used during training and is discarded at test time.

\subsection{Comparative Experiments}

\subsubsection{Comparison with state-of-the-art methods}

Table~\ref{tab:main_results} summarizes the main comparison on the testing set.
% More results on the validation set can be found in \ref{app:validation_results_main}.
\revised{The strong CLIP baseline already surpasses the previously reported best result by a clear margin. Because the external methods are quoted under their original, generally weaker backbones, this margin primarily reflects backbone strength rather than algorithmic superiority, and we therefore do not interpret it as evidence for FDIL itself; the controlled, matched-backbone comparison that isolates the contribution of our mechanisms is reported in the \textit{Matched backbone} group of Table~\ref{tab:main_results} (external methods under the same frozen CLIP ViT-L/14 features) and in Table~\ref{tab:thresholding_test} (method vs.\ calibration). The more relevant observation here is that, once the backbone is sufficiently strong, the remaining challenge is how to map rich visual semantics into abstract and subjective intent labels. Building on the same CLIP features, \textit{UTD only} improves the average F1 over the CLIP baseline (from $53.37_{\pm0.72}$ to $55.68_{\pm0.44}$) while introducing zero extra inference cost, and the complete FDIL framework with lexical-canonical-scenario priors and residual refinement further improves all four reported metrics (average F1 $57.49_{\pm0.49}$). All three in-house rows are averaged over three seeds, so these gains are consistent rather than single-run effects. This pattern is consistent with our motivation: fixed semantic priors provide structured candidate semantics, while uncertainty-aware text supervision and image-aware refinement improve the final intent prediction.}

\subsubsection{Zero-shot Multimodal Large Model Comparison}

\revised{To address the concern that recent multimodal large language models may already solve visual intent recognition through prompting alone, we additionally evaluate zero-shot MLLM baselines using the same Intentonomy label set. The prompt asks the model to analyze the image and return a probability dictionary over selected categories, e.g., \texttt{\{'Category 1': probability, ...\}}. The \textit{Zero-shot VLM} group of Table~\ref{tab:main_results} shows that direct zero-shot MLLM prediction is competitive but remains below the trained CLIP and FDIL models on the test split. GPT-4o reaches $51.52$ average F1 on test, while open-weight Qwen3-VL-8B and InternVL3-8B reach $39.63$ and $39.97$ average F1, respectively. Qwen3-VL-8B and InternVL3-8B are evaluated through OpenAI-compatible vLLM services with the probability-dictionary prompt, and GPT-4o is obtained under the same label-set prompting protocol. These results indicate that stronger general-purpose MLLMs provide useful intent priors, but prompt-only recognition does not remove the need for dataset-specific visual calibration, uncertainty-aware supervision, and image-conditioned refinement.}

\subsubsection{Fair Comparison under a Matched Backbone}

\revised{Table~\ref{tab:main_results} compares against originally reported numbers, where most external methods use weaker backbones. To isolate the contribution of FDIL's mechanisms from backbone strength, we re-implement the core mechanism of representative recent methods over the \emph{same} frozen CLIP ViT-L/14 features, using the same train/val/test split and the same validation-only class-wise threshold search; these results form the \textit{Matched backbone} group of Table~\ref{tab:main_results}, with their mAP and Hard F1 reported in the table note.}

\revised{Two findings emerge. First, once the backbone is equalized, the previously large gaps between these methods and our setting largely close: HLEG and LabCR reach $54.09$ and $53.94$ average F1, essentially matching the plain CLIP baseline ($53.37$), and IntCLIP reaches $52.77$. This confirms that much of their originally reported difference reflects backbone capacity rather than algorithmic mechanism. Second, under this matched protocol FDIL still leads all of them, by $+3.4$ to $+4.7$ average F1, by $+3.4$ to $+5.3$ on threshold-free mAP, and by $+5.7$ to $+8.6$ on the hard subset.}

\revised{As a complementary robustness check, we also replaced the visual backbone with CLIP ViT-L/14 directly inside the external methods' original training pipelines. Naively doing so underperforms the feature-level controlled setting (e.g., HLEG and IntCLIP reach $31.8$ and $43.5$ average F1, while LabCR and PIP-Net overfit severely), because their original optimization recipes are tuned for their native backbones and tend to overfit when a much larger pretrained encoder is fine-tuned on the small Intentonomy training set. We therefore treat the frozen feature-level comparison in the \textit{Matched backbone} group of Table~\ref{tab:main_results} as the fair matched-backbone protocol, and report the original-pipeline retraining only as a robustness observation.}

\subsubsection{Heterogeneous Priors in SLR-C}

\subsubsection{\revised{Method vs. Calibration: A Controlled Decomposition}}

\revised{To separate the contribution of FDIL's mechanisms from the contribution of class-wise calibration, we evaluate both the CLIP baseline and FDIL under matched global and class-wise thresholding, with all thresholds searched on the validation split and all numbers averaged over 3 seeds (Table~\ref{tab:thresholding_test}). Three observations follow.}

\revised{First, \emph{FDIL improves over the CLIP baseline under both thresholding protocols}. Under class-wise thresholding the average-F1 gain is $+4.12$ ($53.37\!\rightarrow\!57.49$) with a $+7.83$ gain on the hard subset, and under global thresholding the gain is $+3.15$ in average F1 and $+7.72$ on the hard subset. The improvement is therefore not an artifact of a particular decision rule.}

\revised{Second, \emph{class-wise thresholding benefits the baseline and FDIL by comparable amounts} ($+2.29$ vs.\ $+3.26$ average F1), so calibration is a shared, method-agnostic post-processing gain rather than something that FDIL uniquely exploits. This directly addresses the concern that the reported improvements might be driven mainly by per-class thresholding: the calibration effect is present for the baseline as well, while FDIL's advantage sits on top of it.}

\revised{Third, and most decisively, the \emph{threshold-free} mAP confirms the same conclusion: FDIL improves mAP from $50.98_{\pm0.49}$ to $55.10_{\pm0.26}$ ($+4.12$), a metric that is invariant to the thresholding choice by construction. We note that the global-threshold F1 metrics carry large seed variance (e.g., hard-subset std up to $\pm3.9$), because a single shared threshold is a brittle operating point; we therefore treat class-wise F1 and mAP as the primary metrics. For completeness, the average-F1 value of $53.37$ for the CLIP baseline corresponds to class-wise thresholding (Avg.\ = mean of Macro/Micro/Samples F1), not mAP. Overall, per-class calibration is an integral part of the final FDIL decision rule, but it is not the source of FDIL's advantage over the baseline.}
